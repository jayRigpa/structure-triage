Before Your Reading: A Guide to the Terms

The instrument you're about to use conducts its conversation in a specific vocabulary. None of it is jargon for its own sake — each term compresses a claim the framework is committed to, and the reading is more useful if the terms are transparent to you rather than obstacles. This guide takes about ten minutes. You don't need to memorize anything: the facilitator will gladly define any term mid-conversation if you ask, and it explains itself reasonably well as it goes. This is orientation, not homework.

Traditional contemplative vocabulary (shamatha, vipassana, jhana, shikantaza, metta, rigpa, koan, pointing-out) is used in its ordinary senses and isn't covered here. What follows are the framework's own coinages.

Where these terms come from

The framework behind this instrument was developed in a multi-stage inquiry into the first principles of awakening, drawing on phenomenological reports across traditions (Zen, Dzogchen, Advaita, Theravada, devotional and apophatic lineages) and on predictive processing — a model from cognitive science in which the mind continuously constructs experience from predictions, and what we call "self" is part of the construction. You don't need to accept predictive processing as true to use the reading; the framework treats all of its models as instruments, not truth-claims (see raft-clause, at the end — it's the most important term here).

Two pieces of predictive-processing vocabulary appear often enough to define:

Prior. A prediction the system holds about how experience will be — often learned long ago, often running outside awareness. Beliefs, emotional patterns, body-level expectations, and the sense of self are all built from priors in this usage. When the facilitator says a prior "filters" experience, it means the prediction shapes what you perceive and how, before anything reaches awareness.

Precision. The weight or confidence assigned to a prior — how much grip it has. Much of what contemplative practice does, in this framework's account, is precision-relaxation: not deleting patterns but reducing their grip so they can be seen as patterns. When a practice "reallocates precision," the felt sense is that something which used to be simply how things are becomes visible as one way things are being constructed.

Transparent vs. opaque. A prior is transparent when you look through it — it structures experience without ever appearing in experience, the way a lens shapes everything seen while remaining invisible. It becomes opaque when you can look at it — it shows up as an object, a noticing: "ah, that's a belief." An opacity event is a moment (small or large) when something transparent becomes visible. Much of awakening, in this account, is the progressive opacification of the self-construction.

Me-tag and author-tag. Two specific priors the framework tracks. The me-tag is the felt label of mine — the tag that makes a thought feel like my thought rather than a thought. The author-tag is the felt sense of being the doer — that actions are initiated by a self behind them. Both can be observed weakening independently of each other.

The core diagnostic vocabulary

Presenting structure. The central term. A structure is a configuration of priors, habits, and protective strategies that is currently organizing a person's practice life — the pattern the reading tries to identify. There are eleven in the catalog. Crucially: structures are not types of people. They are what is currently carrying the weight, they change with development, and most practitioners move through several. The reading maps where you are, not what you are.

Load-bearing. Borrowed from architecture: a load-bearing wall is the one holding the building up — remove it carelessly and things collapse. A load-bearing structure or prior is the one currently doing the real work of holding your experience and identity together. This is why the framework sequences carefully: some load-bearing patterns (especially protective ones) should be renegotiated respectfully, not demolished. Calling something load-bearing is not criticism; it's an engineering observation.

Co-presenting. Two structures active at once. When this happens, sequencing notes determine which is worked first. The framework's firmest sequencing rule: trauma-loaded, parts-dominated systems (Structure 10) are worked first wherever they co-present with anything, because protective priors cannot relax under direct dissolution pressure — the system correctly reads pressure as threat.

Working signature. How you know a practice is actually working — the felt markers of genuine movement, as distinct from pleasant states or good performances. Example: for thought-identified practice, "thoughts arrive with less grip; positions can be held without being defended."

Failure mode. The characteristic way each structure's practice goes subtly wrong while still looking like practice — analysis masquerading as observation, metta sprayed over the avoided from a safe distance, surrender performed by the controller. The failure modes are arguably the most valuable part of each reading; expect the facilitator to name yours.

Switch condition. The specific sign that says: stop this approach, move to that one. Example: "if inquiry into the seeker becomes another acquisition project, switch to service and ethics — orientations that spend the energy outward."

Reinstatement. The return of a previously-seen-through pattern — the "me" reassembling in a triggering context after genuine insight elsewhere. Not a failure; in this framework it's the expected texture of integration, which is held as iterative re-weighting, context by context — each context where identification reinstates is a context not yet visited by the seeing.

Deliberate exposure. Intentionally entering contexts that trigger reinstatement, in order to meet the arising with capacities built elsewhere. The framework endorses this with one strong caveat: a trigger met while fully identified ("blended" — see below) is a rehearsal of the pattern, not a revealing of it. The dose makes the medicine.

Titration. Approaching difficult material in tolerable doses rather than all at once. Central to body- and trauma-related structures.

Craving-policy / resistance-policy. Craving and aversion treated not as feelings but as policies — standing strategies of forcing a desired prior onto experience (craving) or keeping an object out of experience (aversion). The point of the "policy" framing: policies can be caught in the act of running, which feelings cannot.

The five routes

Every practice in the catalog is classified by the route through which it works on construction. Knowing your reading's route helps you understand why the recommended practices are recommended.

Contrast — altered states (absorption, psychedelics, deep stillness) reveal construction by temporarily attenuating it; informative only if the contrast is read as information about construction rather than collected as scenery.

Exhaustion — looking for the self and vividly failing to find it; the engine of self-inquiry and the who-koans. The instruction that matters: the search must fail vividly, not conclude ("there's no self, QED" is thinking, not seeing).

Relaxation — the grip releases; surrender, shikantaza, letting-go paths. Signature risk: the doer surviving as the one who surrenders.

Resolution — attention at fine enough grain that construction is seen assembling; noting and vipassana live here.

Affective — love, devotion, compassion, and grief as solvents; precision transferred to a beloved object until the investor is spent.

Modality-practices (psychedelics, breathwork, dark retreat) are classified separately as amplifiers and perturbations, not standalone paths: powerful destabilizers of construction that supply neither the recognition nor the integration nor the ethics. Their value is conditional on capacity, container, and integration practice — and for trauma-loaded systems they are sequenced late, never first.

The endpoint classes: C, N, T, and W

The framework's most distinctive — and most honest — claim: different practice lineages do not obviously converge on one summit. It identifies four endpoint classes, defined by the traditions' own summit language, and tags practices with the endpoint they historically load toward — so that practice selection is endpoint selection made consciously rather than by lineage default.

C — cessation-oriented. Theravada nibbāna as extinguishment; nirodha-samāpatti; path and fruition. Summit language: cessation, the unconditioned, the deathless, release. World-relation: the ending of becoming.

N — nondual recognition. Dzogchen rigpa; Mahamudra; Advaita self-realization; Kashmir Shaivism's pratyabhijñā; Zen kenshō and its stabilization. Summit language: recognition of the nature of mind, luminous cognizance, One without a second. World-relation: appearances continue, seen as the display of the recognized nature.

T — theistic union. Teresa's spiritual marriage; John of the Cross's living flame; Sufi fanāʾ and baqāʾ — with the apophatic strand (Eckhart, the Cloud of Unknowing, Dionysius) held separately because it behaves differently. Summit language: union, indwelling, annihilation-in-God and subsistence-in-God. World-relation: transfigured relationship. Marked unresolved in the framework: relational union may be the N-attractor stabilized in a personal frame, or a genuinely distinct summit — the framework explicitly declines to adjudicate, and flags that the traditions themselves fork here (some press through union toward annihilation of even the lover; others rest in it).

W — naturalness. Taoist wu wei and ziran; zuowang (sitting-in-forgetfulness); sagehood. Summit language: effortless action, alignment with the Tao, self-so-ness. World-relation: frictionless participation. In readings, W most often appears as the trait-signature that relaxation-route work loads alongside N — the flavor of effort discovered to be optional, of supervision arriving late to actions already underway.

When a reading says a practice "loads C" or "sits at the C–N fork," it is telling you that a real choice is embedded in the technique — one worth making with eyes open. Some practices are tagged neutral: they don't commit you.

Parts vocabulary

The framework incorporates Internal Family Systems (IFS) as a first-class practice, especially for protective and trauma-loaded configurations. Four terms carry weight:

Parts — semi-autonomous subpersonalities; in this framework's translation, clusters of priors with their own agendas, memories, and protective jobs.

Blended / unblending — blended means a part has taken over the seat of identity ("I am furious" rather than "something in me is furious"). Unblending is the shift back to relating to the part rather than being it — and the framework makes a strong identification: unblending IS de-identification, which is why parts-work is not a detour from awakening practice but a form of it, conducted at the pace the system consents to.

Protectors — parts whose job is preventing harm, often via vigilance, control, or avoidance. The framework's rule: protectors relax when their function is honored and their fear is met — voluntarily, never under command.

Exiles — the young, wounded parts the protectors protect.

The late-stage audit vocabulary

If your reading lands at or near Structure 11, four indirect-detection practices will come up. They exist because a transparent prior cannot be attended directly — by definition — so all detection is indirect:

Perturbation — contexts that violate a prior reveal it by the violation: novelty, travel, role reversal, broken routine. The read-out is surprise: anything that startles the system was load-bearing somewhere.

Interpersonal mirrors — others see your filter from outside: contracted feedback relationships, trusted teachers or peers, and coaching others (whose blind spots reliably mirror your own).

The insistence-audit — sweeping for the felt-tones of "obviously," "of course," and "should." Certainty with flavor is a prior with investment. The companion question: "What am I sure of right now, and what does the sureness feel like?"

The affect-asymmetry audit — attending to boredom, faint irritation, mild contempt, subtle hurry: low-grade affects that never quite become objects are the exhaust of filtering priors. What is reliably skimmed past is being kept out of resolution by something.

How the framework talks about its own reliability

Epistemic tags. Claims in the framework carry confidence marks, and the facilitator preserves them in your reading: ESTABLISHED (well-supported),

CONTESTED (serious practitioners and researchers disagree), INFERRED (follows from the model but isn't directly demonstrated), SPECULATIVE (frontier). [PR] marks practitioner report — first-person phenomenological data from the practitioner whose reports helped calibrate the framework, offered as one datapoint, not doctrine. If your reading tags a mechanism INFERRED, that's the framework being honest that the practice effect is better evidenced than the explanation for it.

The raft-clause. Named for the Buddha's simile of the raft: a framework is for crossing, not for carrying. The clause states that this entire model — including the reading you're about to receive — is an instrument, and that the framework's deepest listed failure mode is the framework itself becoming a transparent prior: the lens through which practice is seen, filtering exactly as invisibly as anything it describes. Concretely, this means: use the mapping, test it against your experience, and set it down. If you notice the vocabulary in this guide starting to feel like the truth about your practice rather than one useful way of looking at it — that noticing is the practice.

A last note on the instrument's limits, in its own words: it maps practice structures from self-report in a single conversation. It does not assess or certify attainment — and it will decline if asked. It is not therapy and not a substitute for a teacher. There is no completion signature; the honest expectation is asymptotic.
